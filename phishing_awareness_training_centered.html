What is phishing?
Phishing is a cyberattack where criminals impersonate trusted entities — banks, employers, tech companies — to steal credentials, money, or personal data. It exploits human trust, not software.

Red flags to watch for:
🔗
Suspicious links & domains
Hover before clicking. Watch for misspellings ("paypa1"), unusual TLDs (.ru, .xyz), or extra subdomains.
⏰
Artificial urgency
"Act in 24 hours or lose access" is a manipulation tactic. Legitimate companies rarely threaten immediate closure.
📝
Generic greetings
"Dear Customer" instead of your name signals a mass phishing campaign. Also watch for odd grammar.
🔒
Requests for sensitive info
Legitimate organizations never ask for passwords, OTPs, or card numbers via email.
📎
Unexpected attachments
.zip, .exe, even .docx files can contain malware. Don't open attachments you weren't expecting.
How attackers manipulate you
Social engineering exploits human psychology — not software. Attackers don't hack systems; they hack people.
1
Authority
Impersonating a boss, IT, bank, or government. "This is Microsoft Support — your PC is infected. Give us access now."
2
Scarcity & urgency
"Your account deleted in 2 hours." Time pressure prevents rational thinking and forces rushed decisions.
3
Reciprocity
"We upgraded your plan for free — just verify your details." Offering something to create a sense of obligation.
4
Fear & threats
"We have photos of you. Pay ₹50,000 or we share them." Blackmail designed to paralyze your judgment.
5
Pretexting
A fake recruiter builds trust over weeks, then asks for your Aadhaar "for background verification."
6
Liking & trust
Building rapport before the ask — "I saw your Goa trip post! Anyway, can you approve this wire transfer?"
Your protection checklist
  • ✓
    Verify the sender — Check the full email address. "PayPal Support" can hide <phish@evil.com>.
  • ✓
    Hover before clicking — Preview the real URL in your browser's status bar without any risk.
  • ✓
    Enable MFA — Even if attackers steal your password, multi-factor authentication blocks login.
  • ✓
    Go directly to the source — Type URLs manually. Never click email links for sensitive accounts.
  • ✓
    Call to verify — If a colleague makes an unusual request, call them directly before acting.
  • ✓
    Keep software updated — Patches protect against malware delivered through phishing links.
  • ✓
    Use a password manager — It auto-fills only on the real domain, not lookalike phishing sites.
  • ✓
    Never share OTPs — No bank, government, or company will ever call to ask for a one-time password.
  • ✓
    Report suspicious emails — Use "Report Phishing" in your email client. It protects everyone.
  • ✓
    Slow down — Urgency is the trap. 30 extra seconds of verification could save your data and money.
